# Stage 5: Early Childhood (3 – 5 Years)

**Ava's window:** March 16, 2029 – March 16, 2031

## Key Milestones to Expect

| Domain | Milestone | Typical Range |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Motor (Gross)** | Hops on one foot, catches bounced ball, somersaults, rides bike with training wheels | 3-5 years |
| **Motor (Fine)** | Draws recognizable shapes and people, uses scissors independently, writes some letters, buttons/zips | 4-5 years |
| **Social** | Cooperative play, understands turn-taking, has preferred friends, negotiates conflicts verbally | 3-5 years |
| **Language** | 1500-2000+ words, complex sentences, tells stories, asks "how" and "why" extensively | 3-5 years |
| **Cognitive** | Understands time concepts (yesterday, tomorrow), counts to 20+, recognizes letters, early logical reasoning | 4-5 years |

## Sensory and Motor Protocols

### Gross Motor — Skill Refinement
- **Structured physical activities:** Swimming lessons, gymnastics, martial arts, or dance classes. Structured instruction introduces discipline, following directions, and progressive skill building
- **Unstructured outdoor play:** Minimum 60 minutes daily of free outdoor play (AAP recommendation). Climbing, running, digging, building
- **Balance and coordination:** Bike riding progression (balance bike → training wheels → two wheels), balance beams, hopscotch

### Fine Motor — Pre-Writing and Tool Mastery
- **Handwriting readiness:** Letter tracing, drawing shapes, connecting dots. Focus on grip (tripod grip developing)
- **Art as fine motor work:** Painting with brushes, cutting complex shapes, gluing, assembling
- **Construction:** LEGO/Duplo, building sets, woodworking with safe tools. Spatial reasoning + planning + fine motor precision
- **Self-care independence:** Dressing completely, brushing teeth (supervised), simple food preparation (spreading, pouring, stirring)

## Philosophical Application

### Explicit Teaching and Practice

**Dichotomy of Control — Named and Practiced:**
- The child can now name the concept (use age-appropriate terminology: "Things I can change / Things I can't change")
- **Daily practice:** At dinner or bedtime, discuss one event from the day. "Was there something today that frustrated you? Could you change it? What did you choose to do?"
- Apply to social conflicts: "You can't make Emma share. You can ask nicely. You can choose a different toy. You can wait."
- **Begin teaching the internal component:** "You can't control what happened. You *can* control how you feel about it. Well — you can't stop the feeling from coming, but you can choose what to do with it."
- Introduce the concept through story analysis (see Reading section)

**Articulation — Precision Required:**
- **"Tell me exactly what happened" is now a firm expectation.** Before intervening in any conflict or problem:
  1. "What happened?" (sequence of events)
  2. "How do you feel about it?" (emotional identification)
  3. "What do you want to happen?" (desired outcome)
  4. "What can you do about it?" (dichotomy of control application)
- Do not accept "I don't know" without gentle probing. "Think about it. Take your time. I'll wait."
- Model precise speech yourself. Correct imprecise statements gently: "You said 'everything is ruined.' What specifically is the problem?"

**Local Order — Expanded Responsibility:**
- **Full ownership of personal space.** The child maintains their room, play area, and personal belongings with minimal prompting
- **Contribution to household order:** Simple chores — setting the table, putting away groceries (low shelves), feeding pets, watering plants
- **Planning introduction:** Before starting a project or activity, briefly discuss: "What do we need? Where will we do it? What do we do when we're finished?"
- Connect the principle explicitly: "When your room is messy and you can't find your shoes, how does that feel? When everything is in its place, is it easier?"

## Language and Reading

### Approach: Archetypal Narratives and Moral Analysis

This is the stage where reading transitions from language acquisition to moral and philosophical education.

- **Fables and myths:** Stories with clear moral structures. After each story, discuss:
  - "What did the character do? Why?"
  - "What happened because of that choice?"
  - "Was there something they couldn't control? How did they handle it?"
  - "What would you have done?"
- **Story retelling with analysis:** The child retells the story and explains the moral. Guide but don't dictate
- **Introduce longer chapter books** (read-aloud): Multi-day reading builds sustained attention and narrative memory

### Suggested Books (3-5 years)
- *Aesop's Fables* (illustrated collection — The Tortoise and the Hare, The Boy Who Cried Wolf, The Ant and the Grasshopper)
- *D'Aulaires' Book of Greek Myths* (simplified selections — consequence, hubris, courage)
- *The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe* by C.S. Lewis (read-aloud chapter book — courage, sacrifice, temptation)
- *Charlotte's Web* by E.B. White (friendship, death, legacy)
- *Frog and Toad* series by Arnold Lobel (friendship, responsibility, gentle humor)
- *Anansi the Spider* by Gerald McDermott (trickster tales — cleverness and consequence)
- *The Paper Bag Princess* by Robert Munsch (courage, independent thinking)

## Emotional Regulation: Social Complexity

The child is now navigating friendships, group dynamics, exclusion, fairness, and the full spectrum of social emotions.

**Framework:**
1. **Emotional vocabulary is now rich.** Expect and require precise emotional identification: not just "bad" but "jealous," "embarrassed," "left out," "disappointed"
2. **Problem-solving sequence** (post-emotion):
   - What happened? (facts)
   - How do I feel? (emotion)
   - What can I control? (dichotomy)
   - What do I want? (desired outcome)
   - What can I do? (action plan)
3. **Role-playing:** Practice social scenarios. "What if someone says they don't want to play with you? What could you say? How would you feel? What's in your control?"
4. **Empathy development:** "How do you think she felt when that happened? Why?" Perspective-taking is developing rapidly at this stage

**The "Lying" Phase:**
Children at this age begin to lie — this is actually a cognitive milestone (Theory of Mind). Respond to lying by reinforcing the articulation principle: "I need you to tell me exactly what happened. The truth. Even if it's hard. We can figure out what to do, but only if I know what really happened."

## Environment and Routine

- **Learning space:** Designated area for drawing, reading, puzzles — distinct from play area. Begin the concept of "work space" vs. "play space"
- **Routine independence:** The child follows morning and bedtime routines with minimal prompting. Visual checklists transition from pictures to pictures + words
- **Time awareness:** Use visual timers for transitions and tasks. Introduce the clock — "When the big hand gets to the 6, it's time to clean up"
- **Preparation for school:** If entering preschool/kindergarten, practice the school routine at home. Packing a bag, following a schedule, transitioning between activities on a timer

## Warning Signs (Consult Pediatrician or Specialist)

- Cannot be understood by strangers by age 4
- Cannot follow 3-step instructions by age 4
- Does not engage in cooperative play by age 4
- Cannot hold a crayon or draw basic shapes by age 4
- Persistent difficulty with emotional regulation far beyond peers
- Cannot hop on one foot or catch a ball by age 5
- No interest in letters, numbers, or pre-academic skills by age 5
- Persistent stuttering after age 4 (refer to speech-language pathologist)
