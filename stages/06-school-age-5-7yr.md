## Sensory and Motor Protocols

### Gross Motor — Structured Sport and Physical Discipline
- **Organized activities:** Team or individual sports (swimming, martial arts, soccer, gymnastics). Emphasize effort and improvement over winning
- **Physical challenges:** Encourage activities that push comfort zones — climbing higher, swimming further, running faster. Calibrated risk, not recklessness
- **Daily physical activity:** Minimum 60 minutes moderate-to-vigorous activity (AAP/WHO recommendation)

### Fine Motor — Academic and Practical Application
- **Handwriting:** Progressing from print to fluent writing. Focus on legibility and letter formation
- **Instrument introduction:** Musical instrument instruction develops fine motor precision, bilateral coordination, pattern recognition, and discipline
- **Practical skills:** Basic cooking (measuring, stirring, cutting soft foods), simple tool use (screwdriver, hammer with supervision), sewing (large needle, burlap)
- **Art progression:** Detailed drawing, painting techniques, model building

## Philosophical Application

### Analytical Application Phase

The philosophical framework is now applied analytically to stories, historical events, and the child's own experiences.

**Dichotomy of Control — Applied to Complex Scenarios:**
- **Historical analysis:** When reading about historical figures or events: "What could this person control? What couldn't they control? Did they focus on the right things?"
- **Social application:** School dynamics, friendship conflicts, academic challenges. "You can't control whether you get picked for the team. You can control how hard you practice."
- **Internal sophistication:** Begin distinguishing between emotions (which arise involuntarily) and responses (which are chosen). "The anger showed up. That's normal. What you *did* with it — that's the part that matters."
- **Journaling introduction:** Simple daily journal or reflection practice. "What happened today? What was in my control? What did I choose?"

**Articulation — Dialectic and Defense:**
- **Require defended positions.** Not just "I think X" but "I think X because Y."
- **Encourage disagreement with reasoning.** "I disagree with what the character did because..." is celebrated
- **Reading discussions become analytical:** "Why did the hero make that choice? Was it the right choice? What would have happened if they chose differently?"
- **Written expression:** As writing develops, begin requiring written articulation of ideas — simple book reports, opinion paragraphs, event descriptions
- **Precision correction continues:** "You said 'everyone was mean to me.' Who specifically? What specifically did they do?" — not to dismiss, but to clarify

**Local Order — Full Domain Mastery:**
- **Room, belongings, and schoolwork** are the child's responsibility. Natural consequences for disorder (can't find homework = dealing with the teacher, not the parent rescuing)
- **Time management basics:** The child begins managing their own schedule for homework, play, and chores with a visual planner
- **Expanded household contribution:** Laundry (sorting, folding), meal preparation assistance, cleaning shared spaces
- **The explicit connection:** "You take care of your room. You take care of your schoolwork. You take care of your friendships. That's how you build a life that works."

## Language and Reading

### Approach: Historical Narratives, Biographies, and Adapted Philosophy

Reading is now the primary vehicle for philosophical education. The method becomes highly analytical.

- **Historical narratives:** Real events, real people, real consequences. Discuss motivations, decisions, and outcomes
- **Biographical reading:** Stories of people who overcame adversity, made difficult choices, or failed instructively
- **Analytical discussions:** Every reading session includes:
  - "What was the situation?" (facts)
  - "What did they choose to do?" (action)
  - "What could they control? What couldn't they?" (dichotomy)
  - "Did they tell the truth about the situation, or did they lie to themselves?" (articulation)
  - "What happened as a result?" (consequence)
- **Independent reading:** By age 6-7, the child reads independently daily. Parent reads more complex material aloud for discussion

### Suggested Books (5-7 years)
- *D'Aulaires' Book of Greek Myths* (full — hubris, fate, courage, consequence)
- *Norse Myths* by Kevin Crossley-Holland (adapted — resilience, sacrifice, facing the inevitable)
- *Little House on the Prairie* series by Laura Ingalls Wilder (self-reliance, family, hardship)
- *My Side of the Mountain* by Jean Craighead George (independence, resourcefulness)
- *The Chronicles of Narnia* by C.S. Lewis (full series — moral complexity, courage, temptation)
- *Biographies for young readers:* series on scientists, explorers, leaders (Who Was...? series is accessible)
- *Aesop's Fables* (revisited with deeper analysis — same stories, more sophisticated discussion)
- *The Hobbit* by J.R.R. Tolkien (read-aloud — courage, greed, growth, the unexpected hero)

## Emotional Regulation: Academic Pressure and Social Hierarchy

School introduces new emotional challenges: grades, performance comparison, social hierarchies, potential bullying, and the pressure to conform.

**Framework:**
1. **Academic pressure through the Dichotomy:** "You can't control how hard the test is. You can control how much you prepare. You can't control your grade. You can control your effort."
2. **Social hierarchy:** "You can't control whether everyone likes you. You can control how you treat people. You can control who you choose to spend time with."
3. **Bullying protocol:**
   - Teach the distinction: teasing (occasional, not targeted) vs. bullying (repeated, targeted, power imbalance)
   - For bullying: "This is not something you should handle alone. Tell me exactly what happened. We'll figure out together what to do." — articulation + adult involvement
   - For social difficulty: "What's in your control here? What can you do?"
4. **Failure and mistakes:** Frame as data, not identity. "You failed the spelling test. That tells us what to practice. It doesn't tell us who you are."
5. **Comparison:** "The only person you're competing with is who you were yesterday."

## Environment and Routine

- **Study space:** A quiet, organized desk or table for homework. Consistent location, consistent time. No screens during study time
- **Visual planner/calendar:** Weekly schedule the child helps create. Homework blocks, activity blocks, free time blocks
- **Morning independence:** The child manages their own morning routine with the alarm clock. Natural consequences for tardiness build time management
- **Screen time boundaries:** Clear, consistent rules. Content matters more than duration — educational and creative content prioritized. No screens during meals, before homework completion, or before bed

## Warning Signs (Consult Pediatrician or Specialist)

- Cannot read simple words by end of first grade (age 6-7)
- Persistent difficulty with letter/number reversal after age 7
- Consistent difficulty following multi-step instructions
- Unable to maintain friendships or consistently excluded by peers
- Persistent anxiety about school, separation, or performance
- Difficulty with attention and task completion far beyond peers
- Physical complaints (stomach aches, headaches) without medical cause, especially school-related
- Regression in any previously acquired skill
