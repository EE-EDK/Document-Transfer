# Stage 2: Late Infancy (6 – 12 Months)

**Ava's window:** September 16, 2026 – March 16, 2027

---

## Key Milestones to Expect

| Domain | Milestone | Typical Range |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Visual** | Depth perception maturing, visually tracks fast-moving objects | 6-8 months |
| **Motor (Gross)** | Sits unsupported, crawls, pulls to stand, may take first steps | 6-12 months |
| **Motor (Fine)** | Pincer grasp developing, bangs objects together, points | 8-12 months |
| **Social** | Stranger anxiety, separation anxiety, social referencing (looks to parent for cues) | 7-10 months |
| **Language** | First words ("mama," "dada" with meaning), understands "no," follows simple commands | 9-12 months |
| **Cognitive** | Object permanence established, means-end behavior (pulls cloth to get toy) | 8-12 months |

## Sensory and Motor Protocols

### Gross Motor — Mobility Phase
- **Crawling support:** Create safe, open floor space. Place motivating objects at crawling distance to encourage locomotion
- **Pull-to-stand:** Provide sturdy, stable surfaces (low tables, couches) for pulling up. Never use baby walkers (AAP recommends against them — increased injury risk, may delay walking)
- **Cruising:** Once standing, encourage side-stepping along furniture. Place objects along the route as motivation

### Fine Motor — Precision Phase
- **Pincer grasp development:** Offer small, safe foods (puffs, soft cubes) and small objects for supervised manipulation
- **Stacking and nesting:** Introduce stacking cups, nesting bowls, simple shape sorters
- **Cause-and-effect toys:** Buttons that produce sounds, pop-up toys, simple levers. Reinforces means-end thinking

### Sensory Expansion
- **Water play:** Supervised pouring, splashing, cups of varying sizes during bath time
- **Texture boards:** Mounted panels with varied materials (smooth, rough, bumpy, soft)
- **Sound exploration:** Musical instruments appropriate to age (shakers, drums, xylophones)

## Philosophical Application

### Transition: Modeling to Gentle Redirection

**Dichotomy of Control:**
- Stranger anxiety and separation anxiety emerge. These are normal, healthy attachment behaviors
- Apply the framework through comfort and narration: "Mommy left the room. That feels scary. Mommy always comes back. Let's play while we wait."
- **Do not dismiss the emotion.** Acknowledge it, then redirect to what is within the child's control (choosing a toy, choosing to play)
- When objects are taken away or desired objects are unreachable: "You can't have that one. Which of these can you choose?"

**Articulation:**
- First words emerge. Celebrate and reinforce every attempt
- Begin the earliest form of "requiring communication": when the child points and grunts, model the word. "Ball? You want the ball? Ball." Wait a beat before handing it over — create a brief space for the child to attempt the word
- **Not withholding.** Creating a 2-3 second window for attempt, then providing regardless

**Local Order:**
- "Clean up" transitions become participatory. Hand the child a toy and point to the container. Celebrate placement
- Consistent storage locations for favorite toys. The child begins to know where things belong
- Consistent pre-sleep routine solidifies: same sequence of events in same order every night

## Language and Reading

### Approach: Object Permanence and Cause-Effect in Reading
- **Point and name:** During reading, point to every object and name it. Pause for the child to look or point
- **Lift-the-flap books:** Reinforce object permanence and prediction
- **Simple cause-effect narratives:** "The ball rolled. It went under the chair." Keep narratives grounded in observable physical events

### Suggested Books (6-12 months)
- *Where's Spot?* by Eric Hill (flap book, search narrative)
- *First 100 Words* by Roger Priddy (pointing and naming)
- *The Very Hungry Caterpillar* by Eric Carle (sequence, cause-effect, counting)
- *Moo, Baa, La La La!* by Sandra Boynton (sound association)
- *Goodnight Gorilla* by Peggy Rathbone (visual storytelling with minimal text)

## Emotional Regulation: Separation and Stranger Anxiety

This is the first major emotional regulation challenge. The infant experiences genuine distress at separation from primary caregivers.

**What is happening neurologically:** The infant has developed object permanence (knows the parent exists when out of sight) but has not yet developed the temporal reasoning to predict return. The result is real, intense distress.

**Protocol:**
1. **Acknowledge:** "You're upset because Daddy left. That's okay to feel."
2. **Reassure:** "Daddy always comes back."
3. **Redirect:** "Let's read a book while we wait."
4. **Never sneak away.** Always say goodbye, even if it causes momentary distress. Sneaking away undermines trust and increases anxiety long-term

> See [domains/emotional-regulation.md](../domains/emotional-regulation.md) for full emotional development framework

## Environment and Routine

- **Childproofing is critical** — mobile infant requires thorough safety assessment of all accessible spaces
- **Designated play zones** with clear boundaries. Begin the concept of "this is your space"
- **Consistent daily rhythm:** Predictable sequence of wake, feed, play, nap, play, feed, bedtime routine
- **Introduction to meals:** If starting solids (per pediatrician guidance), create a consistent mealtime environment — same chair, same place, same general time

> See [domains/environment-order.md](../domains/environment-order.md) for space design details

## Warning Signs (Consult Pediatrician)

- Cannot sit with support by 6 months or unsupported by 9 months
- Does not bear weight on legs when held upright by 7-8 months
- No babbling with consonant sounds by 9 months
- Does not respond to own name by 9 months
- No gestures (waving, pointing, reaching) by 12 months
- Loses previously acquired skills at any point
- No pincer grasp by 12 months
