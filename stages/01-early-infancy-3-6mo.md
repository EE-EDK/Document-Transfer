# Stage 1: Early Infancy (3 – 6 Months)

**Ava's window:** June 16, 2026 – September 16, 2026

---

## Key Milestones to Expect

| Domain | Milestone | Typical Range |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Visual** | Full color vision developing, tracks objects smoothly across 180° | 3-4 months |
| **Motor (Gross)** | Rolls front to back, supports weight on forearms, pushes up during tummy time | 3-5 months |
| **Motor (Fine)** | Voluntary grasping begins, reaches for objects, transfers objects hand to hand | 4-6 months |
| **Social** | Laughs aloud, recognizes familiar faces, shows preference for caregivers | 3-5 months |
| **Language** | Babbling begins (consonant-vowel combos: "ba," "da"), turns toward voice | 4-6 months |

## Sensory and Motor Protocols

### Visual Progression
- **Transition to color:** Introduce bold primary-color objects alongside continued high-contrast materials
- **Tracking exercises:** Slowly move colorful objects in arcs across the visual field. Encourage head turning and reaching
- **Depth perception:** Begins developing. Place objects at varying distances during play

### Gross Motor
- **Extended tummy time:** Target 30-60 minutes total daily across sessions
- **Rolling practice:** Place desired toys just out of reach to motivate rolling and pivoting
- **Supported sitting:** Briefly support in seated position to build core strength. Use a Boppy or firm cushion for assisted sitting during play

### Fine Motor Progression
- **Voluntary grasp development:** Offer lightweight rattles, rings, and crinkle toys to encourage intentional reaching and grasping
- **Two-handed play:** Provide objects large enough to require two hands (soft blocks, fabric books)
- **Mouthing:** Expected and necessary — oral exploration is a primary sensory channel at this stage. Ensure all objects are safe for mouthing

## Philosophical Application

### Continued Parental Modeling + Environmental Seeding

**Dichotomy of Control:**
- Continue verbalizing the framework during frustrations. By now, parental patterns should be more established
- Begin applying it to the infant's frustrations: "You can't reach that toy. Let's figure out what you *can* do." (Narration, not expectation of comprehension)
- Tone and emotional resolution patterns are the primary teaching mechanism

**Articulation:**
- Respond to all babbling as communication. Make eye contact, pause, respond conversationally
- "Serve and return" interaction (Harvard Center): when the infant babbles or gestures, treat it as the opening of a dialogue. Respond, pause, wait for the next "serve"
- Name objects consistently. "That's your rattle. Rattle." Repetition builds phonemic association

**Local Order:**
- Introduce the first "clean up" exposure: at the end of play, place toys back in a container while narrating. "We're putting the toys away now. All done."
- Not an expectation — a demonstration of the pattern

## Language and Reading

### Approach: Dialogic Foundations
- **Interactive reading increases:** Pause on pages, point to images, name objects. Wait for any vocal response before continuing
- **Repetition is critical:** Re-read favorite books. Familiarity enables prediction, which is the foundation of comprehension
- **Sound play:** Exaggerate consonant sounds that match early babbling. Repeat baby's sounds back with slight elaboration

### Suggested Books (3-6 months)
- *Dear Zoo* by Rod Campbell (flap book — cause and effect)
- *Brown Bear, Brown Bear, What Do You See?* by Bill Martin Jr. (pattern, repetition, color)
- *Pat the Bunny* by Dorothy Kunhardt (tactile engagement)
- *Peek-a-Baby* by Karen Katz (object permanence introduction)

> See [domains/reading-curriculum.md](../domains/reading-curriculum.md) for full book lists

## Environment and Routine

- **Play space evolution:** Introduce a play gym with hanging objects at varying heights to encourage reaching
- **Object permanence games:** Begin simple peek-a-boo. Partially hide toys under blankets
- **Routine solidification:** Wake, feed, play, sleep cycles should be increasingly predictable (not rigid). The infant begins to anticipate sequences
- **Sound environment:** Introduce varied music genres during play. Name the instruments or styles occasionally

> See [domains/environment-order.md](../domains/environment-order.md) for space design details

## Warning Signs (Consult Pediatrician)

- No laughing or squealing by 5 months
- Cannot hold head steady when supported upright by 4 months
- Does not reach for or grasp objects by 5 months
- No babbling by 6 months
- Does not respond to sounds or turn toward voice by 4 months
- Stiffness or floppiness that seems excessive
- Does not show affection to caregivers or show interest in faces
