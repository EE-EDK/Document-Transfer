## Sensory and Motor Protocols

### Gross Motor — Coordination and Confidence
- **Running and jumping:** Open space play, obstacle courses (pillows, tunnels, low balance beams)
- **Climbing:** Playground structures appropriate to age. Allow supervised risk-taking — calculated risk builds confidence and proprioceptive awareness
- **Tricycle/balance bike:** Develops bilateral coordination, leg strength, spatial navigation
- **Dance and rhythm:** Movement to music develops timing, coordination, and body awareness

### Fine Motor — Precision and Tool Use
- **Drawing progression:** From scribbles to intentional circles and lines. Provide diverse media (crayons, markers, finger paint, chalk)
- **Scissors:** Child-safe scissors with supervision. Cutting develops bilateral coordination and hand strength
- **Threading and lacing:** Large beads on string, lacing cards. Builds pincer precision and patience
- **Playdough/clay:** Rolling, pinching, cutting, shaping. Excellent for hand strength and creative expression
- **Self-care tasks:** Buttoning large buttons, zipping zippers, pulling on socks. Motor development + autonomy

## Philosophical Application

### Direct Teaching Begins

This is the pivotal stage. The child has sufficient language and cognitive capacity for the philosophical framework to move from background modeling to foreground teaching.

**Dichotomy of Control — Explicit Introduction:**
- Use simple, concrete language: "Can you change the rain? No. Can you choose what to play inside? Yes."
- Practice with daily low-stakes scenarios. Make it a game:
  - "The block tower fell. Can you control that it fell? What can you control?"
  - "Your friend took the red crayon. Can you make them give it back? What can you choose?"
- **"What can you do about it?"** becomes a household phrase — used consistently by all caregivers
- Create a visual aid: a simple two-column chart (with pictures) — "Things I can't change" / "Things I can choose"

**Articulation — Progressive Precision:**
- **"Use your words" evolves to "Tell me exactly what happened."**
- When the child says "He's mean!" respond with: "What did he do? Tell me what happened."
- Model precision yourself. Instead of "Be good," say "Please walk, don't run, in the store."
- Expand the child's statements: Child says "Want that" → "Can you tell me which one? The blue one or the green one?"
- **Begin the rule:** Before intervention in a dispute or problem, the child must attempt to describe it. Help them articulate, but require the attempt

**Local Order — Ownership Begins:**
- **The child's room/play area is now their responsibility** (with guidance and help)
- Establish clear, achievable expectations: toys in bins, books on shelf, dirty clothes in hamper
- "Before we start something new, we put away what's out" — consistent, gentle, firm
- Involve the child in organizing: let them choose which bin for which toys. Ownership of the system builds investment in maintaining it
- **Morning and bedtime routines** are now partially self-directed with picture checklists

## Language and Reading

### Approach: Narrative Comprehension and Moral Introduction
- **Dialogic reading is fully active:** "Why do you think the bear is sad?" "What's going to happen next?" "What would you do?"
- **Story retelling:** After reading, ask the child to tell you what happened. Accept approximations. Model expanded versions
- **Introduce consequence narratives:** Stories where characters make choices and experience results. Begin linking character actions to outcomes

### Suggested Books (18-36 months)
- *The Gruffalo* by Julia Donaldson (courage, cleverness, consequence)
- *No, David!* by David Shannon (boundaries, rules, unconditional love)
- *Corduroy* by Don Freeman (perseverance, acceptance)
- *Owl Babies* by Martin Waddell (separation, trust, return)
- *Aesop's Fables: A Pop-Up Book* (simplified — first moral framework in story form)
- *The Little Engine That Could* by Watty Piper (perseverance, self-talk)
- *Where the Wild Things Are* by Maurice Sendak (emotional exploration, return to safety)

## Emotional Regulation: Autonomy Crisis and Defiance

The "terrible twos" (which extend well into three) represent the child's first systematic assertion of will. This is healthy and necessary.

**Framework:**
1. **Distinguish defiance from inability.** A child who *won't* is different from a child who *can't*. Respond accordingly
2. **Offer controlled choices:** "You need to wear shoes. Red shoes or blue shoes?" — preserves autonomy within necessary boundaries
3. **Name the emotion:** "You're angry because you wanted to stay at the park." Emotional vocabulary is being built now — name it precisely
4. **Hold the boundary calmly:** "I understand you're upset. We are still leaving the park." Firm, not angry. The boundary doesn't move, but the emotion is acknowledged
5. **Post-event processing:** After calm returns, briefly revisit: "You were upset about leaving. Could you control when we had to leave? No. But you chose to walk to the car yourself. That was a good choice."

**Emotional Vocabulary Expansion:**
Move beyond "happy/sad/angry." Introduce: frustrated, disappointed, excited, nervous, proud, embarrassed, confused, surprised. Use these words in context throughout the day, not just during emotional events.

## Environment and Routine

- **Prepare for potty training:** Child-sized potty, accessible location, no pressure. Follow the child's readiness signals (staying dry for 2+ hours, interest in the toilet, discomfort with wet diapers)
- **Independence infrastructure:** Step stools, child-height hooks and shelves, accessible snack area (with approved options)
- **Routine charts evolve:** Add more steps, let the child move a marker or magnet to track progress through the routine
- **Transition warnings:** "Five more minutes, then we clean up." "Two more minutes." Predictable transitions reduce meltdowns

## Warning Signs (Consult Pediatrician)

- Fewer than 50 words by 24 months
- No two-word combinations by 24 months
- Cannot follow two-step instructions by 24 months ("Pick up the ball and bring it here")
- Does not engage in pretend play by 24 months
- Cannot run or climb stairs by 24 months
- Consistent regression in any domain
- No interest in other children by 30 months
- Speech is largely unintelligible to strangers by 36 months
