## Key Milestones to Expect

| Domain | Milestone | Typical Range |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| **Motor (Gross)** | Walking independently, squatting to pick up toys, climbing stairs with help | 12-15 months |
| **Motor (Fine)** | Stacking 2-3 blocks, scribbling with crayon, turning pages (several at a time) | 12-18 months |
| **Social** | Parallel play (plays alongside peers, not with), imitates adult actions, shows empathy basics | 12-18 months |
| **Language** | 10-50 words, follows simple instructions ("bring me the cup"), points to request | 12-18 months |
| **Cognitive** | Symbolic play begins (pretends to feed doll), identifies body parts, sorts by shape | 14-18 months |

## Sensory and Motor Protocols

### Gross Motor — Walking and Climbing
- **Walking practice:** Varied surfaces (grass, carpet, wood floor, sand). Barefoot when safe — proprioceptive feedback is essential for balance development
- **Climbing:** Low, safe climbing structures. Supervised stair practice. Climbing develops core strength, spatial awareness, and risk assessment
- **Ball play:** Rolling, kicking attempts, throwing. Develops coordination, timing, bilateral integration

### Fine Motor — Intentional Manipulation
- **Large crayons/markers:** First scribbling. Provide large paper surfaces (butcher paper taped to table or floor)
- **Stacking:** Blocks, cups, rings. Builds hand-eye coordination and spatial reasoning
- **Simple puzzles:** Knob puzzles with 3-5 pieces. Shape sorters with basic shapes (circle, square, triangle)
- **Pouring and scooping:** Sand table, water play, dry rice bins. Develops wrist rotation and bilateral coordination

## Philosophical Application

### Active Redirection Phase

**Dichotomy of Control:**
- Tantrums begin as the child's desires outpace their capabilities and communication. This is the first real testing ground
- **During a tantrum:** Stay calm. Get at eye level. "You're frustrated. You wanted X. You can't have X right now. You *can* choose Y or Z."
- Do NOT reason during peak emotional arousal. Wait for the wave to crest. Then apply the framework
- Model the framework with your own frustrations visibly: "The car won't start. I can't control that. I can call for help."

**Articulation:**
- **The "word before action" principle begins.** When the child points and grunts, model the word and wait 3-5 seconds for an attempt before providing
- Celebrate all approximations. "Ba" for ball is celebrated. Then gently model: "Ball! Yes, ball."
- Do NOT withhold necessities (food, comfort, safety) pending verbalization. This applies to wants, not needs
- Expand single words into phrases: Child says "milk" → Parent says "You want milk? More milk, please."

**Local Order:**
- **"Clean up before new activity" rule begins.** Gently enforced with participation. Sing a clean-up song. Make it routine, not punishment
- Designated spaces for designated activities: eating at the table, drawing at the desk, toys in the play area
- The child participates in putting shoes by the door, placing items in bins

## Language and Reading

### Approach: Vocabulary Explosion Support
- **Name everything.** Narrate constantly. "You're walking to the kitchen. There's the dog. The dog is brown."
- **Dialogic reading intensifies:** Point and ask "What's that?" Wait for response. Accept approximations. Model the correct word
- **Simple choices:** "Do you want the red cup or the blue cup?" Builds vocabulary and agency simultaneously

### Suggested Books (12-18 months)
- *First 100 Animals* by Roger Priddy (pointing, naming)
- *From Head to Toe* by Eric Carle (body parts, imitation)
- *Llama Llama Red Pajama* by Anna Dewdney (separation anxiety, emotional naming)
- *Go, Dog. Go!* by P.D. Eastman (prepositions, action words)
- *Press Here* by Herve Tullet (cause-effect, interactive)

## Emotional Regulation: Tantrums and Autonomy

The toddler is experiencing a fundamental tension: the desire for autonomy with insufficient capability and communication to achieve it. Tantrums are the result.

**Framework:**
1. **Safety first** — ensure the child cannot hurt themselves or others
2. **Stay present** — do not abandon or isolate during emotional flooding
3. **Acknowledge the emotion:** "You are really angry right now."
4. **Wait for de-escalation** — do not try to reason or redirect during peak arousal
5. **Apply the framework post-peak:** "You wanted the cookie. You couldn't have it. That was frustrating. What can we do instead?"
6. **Consistency is the tool.** Same response every time builds the neural pathway

**Do NOT:**
- Punish emotional expression
- Give in to tantrums to end them (teaches escalation as a strategy)
- Shame ("Big girls don't cry")
- Isolate ("Go to your room until you calm down") — the child cannot self-regulate yet and needs co-regulation

## Environment and Routine

- **Child-height accessibility:** Low hooks for coats, step stools in bathroom, low shelves for toys. Enables autonomy within order
- **Routine chart** (pictures): Visual sequence for morning and bedtime routines. Even pre-literate children benefit from picture-based sequence cues
- **Meal independence:** Appropriate utensils, plate, cup. Accept mess as a feature of learning, not a failure of order

> See [domains/environment-order.md](../domains/environment-order.md) for space design details

## Warning Signs (Consult Pediatrician)

- Not walking by 18 months
- Fewer than 6 words by 18 months
- Does not point to show things of interest
- Does not imitate actions or words
- Loses previously acquired skills
- Does not follow simple verbal instructions
- No symbolic/pretend play by 18 months
