# Language and Communication Development

Speech development protocols, dialogic reading methods, and the progressive articulation framework.

---

## Language Acquisition Timeline

### Receptive Language (Understanding)

| Age | Milestone |
|-----|-----------|
| 0-3 months | Startles to sound, calms to voice, recognizes caregiver voices |
| 3-6 months | Turns toward sound source, responds to tone of voice |
| 6-9 months | Responds to own name, understands "no," recognizes familiar words |
| 9-12 months | Follows simple commands with gestures ("give me"), understands ~50 words |
| 12-18 months | Points to named objects, follows simple verbal commands without gestures |
| 18-24 months | Follows two-step commands ("pick up the ball and bring it here"), understands 200+ words |
| 2-3 years | Understands prepositions (in, on, under), pronouns, simple questions |
| 3-5 years | Understands complex sentences, time concepts, "why" explanations |
| 5-7 years | Understands figurative language, idioms, complex instructions |

### Expressive Language (Production)

| Age | Milestone |
|-----|-----------|
| 0-3 months | Cooing, vowel sounds, differentiated cries |
| 3-6 months | Babbling (consonant-vowel: "ba," "da"), vocal play, laughing |
| 6-9 months | Reduplicated babbling ("bababa," "mamama"), gesture + vocalization |
| 9-12 months | First words with meaning, jargoning (babble with sentence-like intonation) |
| 12-18 months | 10-50 words, mostly nouns, pointing + word combinations |
| 18-24 months | 50-200+ words, two-word combinations ("more milk," "daddy go") |
| 2-3 years | 200-1000+ words, 3-4 word sentences, asks questions, uses pronouns |
| 3-5 years | 1500-2000+ words, complex sentences, tells stories, uses past tense |
| 5-7 years | 5000-10000+ words, complex grammar, narratives, reads independently |

## Dialogic Reading Method

**Source:** National Reading Panel (NICHD, 2000) — identified as a highly authoritative method for accelerating language acquisition.

### Core Technique: PEER

| Step | Action | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| **P**rompt | Ask a question about the page | "What is the dog doing?" |
| **E**valuate | Assess the child's response | (Child says "running") |
| **E**xpand | Extend the response | "Yes! The brown dog is running fast through the park." |
| **R**epeat | Ask the child to repeat the expanded version | "Can you say 'the dog is running'?" |

### Prompting Types (CROWD)

| Type | Description | Example | Best Age |
|------|-------------|---------|----------|
| **C**ompletion | Leave a blank for the child to fill | "Brown bear, brown bear, what do you ___?" | 18 months+ |
| **R**ecall | Ask about something earlier in the book | "What happened to the caterpillar on Monday?" | 2.5 years+ |
| **O**pen-ended | Ask for the child to describe in their own words | "What's happening on this page?" | 2 years+ |
| **W**h-questions | Who, what, where, when, why | "Where is the mouse hiding?" | 18 months+ |
| **D**istancing | Connect the book to the child's life | "Have you ever felt scared like the owl babies?" | 3 years+ |

### Dialogic Reading by Stage

**0-12 months:** Not truly dialogic yet. Focus on:
- Reading aloud for prosody and phoneme exposure
- Pointing to pictures, naming objects
- Allowing the infant to look and touch
- Pausing at page turns to create rhythm

**12-24 months:** Early dialogic:
- "What's this?" (pointing to objects)
- Accept any vocalization as a response
- Name and expand: Child points → "That's a cat. The cat is sleeping."
- Let the child turn pages and direct attention

**24-36 months:** Active dialogic:
- Full PEER sequence with simple prompts
- Completion prompts with familiar books
- Wh-questions (what, where, who)
- Begin recall questions for familiar stories

**3-5 years:** Advanced dialogic:
- Open-ended questions dominate
- Distancing questions connect to real life
- "Why" and "how" questions
- Predictions: "What do you think will happen next?"
- Character analysis: "Why did she do that? How does he feel?"

**5-7 years:** Analytical reading:
- Full moral and philosophical analysis (see [PHILOSOPHY.md](../PHILOSOPHY.md))
- Compare and contrast characters and situations
- Defend opinions with evidence from the text
- Connect themes across different books
- Written responses to reading

## The Articulation Framework

### Principle
Demanding progressive precision in speech as vocabulary expands. Aligning cognitive processing with language articulation to reduce emotional chaos and enable effective problem resolution.

**Source:** Peterson, J.B.; supported by NICHD research on expressive language and behavioral regulation.

### Progressive Precision by Stage

**12-18 months:** Create space for communication
- When child points/grunts, model the word, wait 2-3 seconds
- Provide regardless of whether the child attempts the word
- Goal: establish that communication has power

**18-24 months:** Word before action
- Require an attempt at a word before fulfilling a want (not a need)
- Accept any approximation
- "Can you say 'more'?" Wait. "Mo? Yes! More crackers!"

**24-36 months:** Sentence formation
- Encourage expanding single words into phrases
- "Milk" → "Do you want milk? Can you say 'want milk'?"
- Model full sentences constantly

**3-5 years:** Precision and specificity
- "Tell me exactly what happened" — require sequential narration
- "He was mean" → "What did he do specifically?"
- "I don't like it" → "What specifically don't you like? Why?"
- The four-question framework:
  1. What happened?
  2. How do you feel?
  3. What do you want?
  4. What can you do?

**5-7 years:** Argument and defense
- "I think X because Y"
- Require evidence for claims
- Encourage respectful disagreement
- Written articulation begins
- Precision correction continues: "everyone" → "who specifically?"

### Guardrails
- **Never punitive.** A child struggling to articulate is helped, not withheld from
- **Calibrate to readiness.** A 2-year-old cannot produce a full sentence on demand. Accept approximations appropriate to stage
- **Need vs. want distinction.** Needs (food, comfort, safety, medical) are never withheld pending verbalization. Wants (specific toy, specific activity) can have a brief communication window
- **Frustration threshold.** If the child is becoming distressed by the communication demand, provide immediately and model the language after: "You wanted the blue truck. Blue truck!"

## Language-Rich Environment Checklist

### Daily Practices (All Ages)
- [ ] **Narrate constantly** — describe what you're doing, seeing, hearing
- [ ] **Read aloud** — minimum 15-20 minutes daily, ideally 2-3 sessions
- [ ] **Respond to all communication** — babbles, gestures, words, sentences
- [ ] **Expand utterances** — add 1-2 words to whatever the child says
- [ ] **Wait time** — pause 3-5 seconds after questions to allow processing
- [ ] **Avoid baby talk** — use correct words and grammar (simplified, not incorrect)
- [ ] **Sing songs** — nursery rhymes, counting songs, action songs
- [ ] **Limit screen time** — screens are passive; language requires interaction (AAP guidelines)

### Environmental Supports
- Books accessible at child height in multiple rooms
- Label items in the environment (post-toddler: word + picture labels)
- Reduce background noise during reading and conversation (TV off)
- Create conversation opportunities (meals together, car rides, walks)
